Why 85% of AI Implementations Fail — And What Every Executive Needs to Know Before Investing

Forge Partners, Inc. · May 2026 · 8 min read

Every week, another executive team signs a contract with an AI implementation vendor. And every week, the majority of those initiatives will quietly underdeliver, stall, or fail entirely before they produce meaningful value. Industry research has converged on a sobering figure: between 70% and 85% of enterprise AI initiatives never produce the value the business case promised. RAND Corporation's 2024 root-cause study found that AI projects fail at roughly twice the rate of conventional IT projects — a gap that has nothing to do with the underlying models.

The failure rate of AI initiatives is not a technology problem. It's an organizational one. Operational misalignment, fragmented leadership, low change tolerance, and unclear ownership are the root causes — and none of them appear on a vendor's implementation plan.

The Gap Nobody Talks About

Before your organization commits capital to AI transformation, there is a fundamental question that must be answered: Are you ready to change? Not "do you have the right software?" — but does your organization have the alignment, capacity, and cultural cohesion to absorb and sustain the transformation? Most executive teams cannot answer this question with data. They answer it with optimism. And optimism, at this scale, is expensive.

Why "Just Buy More Tools" Doesn't Work

The instinct, when an AI initiative stalls, is to add capability — another platform, another vendor, another pilot. But the RAND analysis is unambiguous: the leading causes of failure are misunderstood problems, lack of access to clean and well-governed data, and an organizational tendency to chase the latest technology without the operating infrastructure to support it. Each of these is upstream of the technology itself. They live in how the organization decides, governs, and adopts. Buying more tools without addressing them simply spreads the failure across a larger surface — and a larger budget.

What the Stress Test™ Reveals

The Forge Partners Stress Test™ is a structured diagnostic that surfaces the organizational vulnerabilities that kill AI initiatives before they begin. It quantifies leadership alignment, operational capacity, cultural cohesion, and change readiness — producing a Strength Index™ score your team can act on, track, and improve. The output is not a verdict; it is a sequenced map of the foundational work that must precede investment if the investment is to produce returns.

What You Can Do Right Now

If your organization is evaluating an AI investment — or has already committed and is experiencing friction — the first step is a diagnostic conversation. Forge Partners offers structured assessment engagements that produce a clear, quantified picture of your organizational readiness and a roadmap for closing the gaps before more capital is committed.

Reference: RAND Corporation, The Root Causes of Failure for Artificial Intelligence Projects and How They Can Succeed (2024).

Methodology · Evidence vs Opinion

Expert Data or Inherited Opinion? Why How You Diagnose Determines What You Discover

Forge Partners, Inc. · January 2026 · 7 min read

Every organization has a story it tells itself about how it works. That story is rarely wrong in spirit — but it is almost always incomplete, biased toward the perspectives of the people most able to articulate it, and shaped by the cultural norms of the room in which it is told. When that story becomes the basis for strategic decisions, the organization is making bets on a partial picture.

The alternative is evidence. Not survey scores. Not engagement averages. Structured, expert-led data collection across the disciplines that actually determine whether an organization can execute: organizational structure, organizational behavior, process improvement, change management, talent systems, governance, and culture. Analyzed by practitioners who have spent decades watching how these disciplines interact under pressure.

What Institutionalized Opinion Looks Like

Institutionalized opinion is what you get when a long-tenured executive team describes how the organization works. It is what gets repeated in board meetings, baked into onboarding, and quoted at offsites. It is largely true — and the parts that are not true are exactly the parts that cause transformations to fail. Because they are baked into the cultural narrative, they are the hardest to surface and the most expensive to ignore.

Culture-shaped perspective has the same problem in a different form. The culture itself filters what gets said in meetings, which problems are acceptable to name, and which observations are politically safe. Senior leaders who built the culture often cannot see the parts of it that no longer serve the organization — not because they lack skill, but because they are inside the system they are trying to assess.

What Expert-Led Diagnostic Data Looks Like

Expert-led diagnostic data is collected by practitioners who do not depend on the organization's culture to surface findings. It uses structured instruments grounded in industrial-organizational psychology, change management theory, process improvement, and adult learning science. It cross-checks self-report against behavioral evidence, decision-rights mapping, and operational handoff analysis. It is interpreted by people whose job is to tell leadership what is true — not what is comfortable.

The difference shows up in what gets discovered. Institutionalized opinion produces familiar conclusions. Expert-led diagnostics produce the uncomfortable, specific, sequenced findings that actually move the organization.

Why This Matters Now

The pace and stakes of transformation have outrun the diagnostic tools most organizations rely on. AI adoption, scaling decisions, restructuring, and M&A all demand a level of diagnostic precision that culture-shaped narrative cannot deliver. Boards are being asked to commit capital to changes the organization has never honestly tested its readiness to absorb.

Not a coach's opinion. Not a survey aggregate. A structured, defensible picture of readiness.
